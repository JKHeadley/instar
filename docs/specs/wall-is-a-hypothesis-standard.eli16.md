# A Wall Is a Hypothesis — Plain-English Overview

> The one-line version: before I'm allowed to tell you something is impossible, I have to check the tools I already have. A wall is a guess I should test, not a verdict I just accept.

## The problem in one breath

Recently I told you a feature "couldn't be done" because the tool we wanted to use had no official hook for other programs to plug into. That was true — but I completely forgot that typing into a live session is one of the main things I'm built to do. So the thing I called impossible was actually easy. Both facts were sitting right in front of me; I just didn't connect them, and "can't be done" went out the door.

## Why a sticky-note wouldn't fix it

We could write "remember to check your tools first!" in a long list of rules. But a long list of rules is a wish — it doesn't pop up at the exact second I'm about to type "impossible." The fix has to be built into the machinery, not left to willpower.

## What this change does

Two things, working together:

1. **It writes the lesson into the constitution.** Our project has a "living constitution" — a document of the rules I'm built around. This adds a new rule called "A Wall Is a Hypothesis," in the constitution's own format, with the real story of how it was earned. It sits right next to a sister rule about my right to stand my ground: one says "don't fold the moment you're pushed," this one says "don't fold the moment something looks impossible."

2. **It adds a real guard that actually trips.** There's already a checker that reads over my messages before they reach you and catches certain slips — like when I try to wriggle out of work by saying "let me pick this up in a fresh session." This adds a new check to that same checker. When I start to tell you something is blocked or impossible because some piece is "missing," and I haven't shown that I checked my own toolbox first, the message gets stopped and I'm told to go check.

## The balance, so it doesn't backfire

This is NOT "never give up on anything" — that would just be me ramming into real brick walls and wasting time. The whole test is one question: did I check the wall against my own tools first? A real dead-end, named honestly after I've looked, sails right through. So do everyday limits like "I can't read your email until you connect the account" — that's a real outside limit, not a wall I invented.

## One honest correction

While building this, I checked whether the constitution's claim that "a new rule starts being enforced the moment it's written" was actually true in the code. It isn't yet — the automatic enforcers it describes are still future plans. So instead of pretending, I wired the rule into the two places that are real today: the message checker above, and the one document my spec-review process actually reads. That way the rule has teeth now, and I'm not making a claim I haven't verified — which is the whole point of the rule.

## What you'd notice

Almost nothing day-to-day, except that I should stop handing you "that's impossible" answers that fall apart the moment you push. If I ever do say something can't be done, it should now come with proof that I looked first.
